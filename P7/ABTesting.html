A/B Testing Udacity Free Trial Screener

Experiment Design

Metric Choice

List which metrics you will use as invariant metrics and evaluation metrics here.

 

  • Invariant metrics: number of cookies, number of clicks, click-through probability
  • Evaluation metrics: retention, gross conversion, net conversion

For each metric, explain both why you did or did not use it as an invariant metric and why you did or did not use it as an evaluation metric. Also, state what results you will look for in your evaluation metrics in order to launch the experiment.

  • Number of cookies: (number of unique cookies to visit course overview page) The unit of diversion, expect it to be evenly distributed amongst the control and experiment groups.

  • Number of clicks: (number of unique cookies to click free trial button) Good invariant metric as the user will click before starting the experiment.

  • Click-through probability: (unique cookies to click the "start free trial" button per unique cookies to view the course overview page). Chosen as an invariant metric as the user will click before starting the experiment.

  • Retention: (number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14 days trial period and make their first payment per number of user-ids who enrolled in the free trial)  Good evaluation metric as it shows how many users remained enrolled after the trial period. However, because the unit of analysis is the number of students which enrolled, we need a higher amount of pageviews to be able to compute this metric. This would take too long and would not satisfy the time requirements of the client. (Total Page Views 4,741,212)

  • Gross conversion: (number of user-ids to complete checkout and enroll in the free trial per unique cookie to click the "start free trial" button)  Chosen as an evaluation metric as dependent of the effect of the experiment and shows whether we managed to decrease the cost of enrollment that will not be paying customers.

  • Net conversion (number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14 day trial, making at least one payment, per the number of unique cookies to click the "start free trial" button)  Chosen as an evaluation metric as directly dependent on the outcome of the experiment, shows positive financial outcome of the change.

  • Number of user-ids: (number of user-ids enrolled in trial).  This is not a suitable invariant metric and while it could be used as an evaluation metric, it is not ideal. User-ids are tracked only after enrolling in the free trial and equal distribution between the control and experimental branches would not be expected. User-id count could be used to evaluate how many enrollments stayed beyond the 14 day free trial boundary, but it is not normalised, therefore it is not used here.

I will use Gross and Net Conversion.  The expectation for the experiment will be: Gross conversion will demonstrate whether costs will be lowered by introducing the screener, net conversion will demonstrate how introducing this change will show financial change.  To launch, I will require Gross conversion to have a practically significant decrease, and Net conversion not to decrease.

Retention

  • Baseline Conversion: 53%
  • Minimum Detectable Effect: 1%

  • total sample size = 78,230 enrollments

Using retention as evaluation metric will make the experiment go much longer (4,741,212 page views will take around 4 months on full site traffic). Therefore use Gross and Net Conversion as evaluation metrics.

Pageview per group: 27,413 / 0.08 (clicks/pageview) = 342663

Total pageview: 342663 *2 (no of groups) = 685326

Bonferroni correction was not used.  The number of page views needed will be 685,326 to power the experiment appropriately.

Summary

State whether you used the Bonferroni correction, and explain why or why not. If there are any discrepancies between the effect size hypothesis tests and the sign tests, describe the discrepancy and why you think it arose.

Potential students were split into experimental and control groups.  For validation and sanity checking purposes the number of cookies, number of clicks and click-through probability were selected.  Gross and Net conversion were chosen as evaluation metrics.  The null hypothesis is that there is no difference between the evaluation metrics between the two groups; a practical significance threshold was set for each metric.  

For launching the experiment the requirement is that the null hypothesis must be rejected for ALL evaluation metrics and that the difference between branches must at least exceed the practical significance threshold.  The Bonferroni correction was not used here because our acceptance requires statistically significant differences for ALL evaluation metrics.  

Bonferroni correction is a method for controlling for false positives (concluding that a significant difference is present when it is not) when using multiple metrics in which relevance of ANY of the metrics matches the hypothesis - the risk of false positives increases as the number of metrics increase.  In this experiment ALL metrics must be applied at launch, the risk of false negatives increases as the number of metrics increase, so the controlling of false positives is not within our acceptance criteria.

Gross conversion was statistically and practically significant.  Gross conversion rate dropped in the experimental group, therefore the screener was effective at reducing the number of students that enrolled from the initial click.  Net conversion was not statistically or practically significant.  Net conversion was reduced suggesting that the screener had a negative effect on student numbers completing the 14-day trial.  It deterred some students from enrolling that would have otherwise completed the trial.  This does not support the hypothesis.

Recommendation

Make a recommendation and briefly describe your reasoning.

Gross and Net Conversion were used as metrics. Gross conversion give us the probability to enroll given a click; it indicates whether or not the screener is effective at filtering out unprepared students. We also measure net conversion to see the impact of the filter on completion of the free trial and eventual payment. 

Gross conversion was statistically and practically significant. Gross conversion rate dropped in the experimental group, therefore the screener was effective at reducing the number of students that enrolled from the initial click. Net conversion was not statistically or practically significant. Net conversion was reduced suggesting that the screener had a negative effect on student numbers completing the 14-day trial. It deterred some students from enrolling that would have otherwise completed the trial. This does not support the hypothesis.

The feature should therefore not be implemented or it should be tested again with other possible designs.

Follow-Up Experiment

Give a high-level description of the follow up experiment you would run, what your hypothesis would be, what metrics you would want to measure, what your unit of diversion would be, and your reasoning for these choices.

Udacity currently uses pop-ups when the user is logged onto the site.  This is done to get the users opinion on course content and whether they would recommend the site to others etc.  In order to encourage a stronger relationship and to motivate and encourage the users on the trial, a similar approach could be used to monitor the users usage of the site.  If the user was not following the guidelines on the number of recommended hours, then a pop-up could be used to encourage the user with messages and links to motivational material such as previous udacity users who have gone on to find good jobs (these pages currently exist on the site).

The use of such a pop-up may encourage the students on the 14-day trial and reduce the number who do not continue onto full membership.  Therefore the retention rate would be the best evaluation metric.  User-id will be the unit of diversion and the number of user-ids to enroll in the course would be the invariant metric as we could easily split the pop-up usage evenly amongst the experimental and control group. This test should also be fairly low cost as many of the resources are already used at Udacity.
